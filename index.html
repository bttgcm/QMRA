Introduction

While epidemiological studies represent the gold standard to understand health risk associated with environmental exposure, Quantitative Microbial Risk Assessment (QMRA) is an alternative method that has gained popularity in recent years.

QMRA

Letâ€™s first understand what Quantitative Microbial Risk Assessment (QMRA) means. The method helps quantify the risk caused by a pathogen(s) in a given situation.

As an example, we will look at the risk of illness while swimming in a river.

Step 1: Hazard Identification

In this step a specific hazard is identified for the analysis. This is usually related to a situation or a target group, it involves a pathway and one or more pathogens.

In the example, as a pathway we will consider involuntary ingestion of contaminated water during swimming. Our target group will be swimmers and as a pathogen, we will consider Adenovirus. The choice of the pathogen can be determined based on multiple criteria, including frequency of infection, specific interest in a particular pathogen, and decay in the environment.

Step 2: Exposure assessment

This step quantifies the exposure of the target population (the swimmers) to the hazard (ingestion of Adenoviruses). The result is the quantification of a dose of pathogen that is ingested by the target.

Drawing on a previous study, to quantify the dose involuntary ingested by a swimmer two data are needed: the involuntary ingestion and the concentration of viruses. The involuntary ingestion per 45 min swim is assumed to be 20 ml of water. The concentration is estimated using field data as 1 gene copy per 100ml.

The dose is therefore estimated as:

dose = ingestion x concentration

In the example, the average ingestion would be 0.2 virus per swim.Try it yourself:

Step 3: Dose response

Using dose-response curves we can estimate the probability of infection given a certain dose. These curves are based on experimental data and are determined for each specific pathogen. Step 4: Risk characterization

This step combines all the previous ones to characterize the risk of infection over a period of time. In our example this refers to the risk of infection from ingesting river water containing Adenoviruses. Previously we have estimated the risk for a single event at 2%. It could be possible to estimate the risk for a person going on a daily swim during a month (therefore 30 events), this estimate will be at 45%.

With the previously calculated risk per swim event we could also have an initial estimate of how many people could get sick in a hot summer day where 500 swimmers are present. This would simply be 500 x 2% = 10.

Step 5: risk mitigation

One of the uses of QMRA is to evaluate mitigation strategies. This means that we could estimate the risk of infection for a swimmer, over the period of one month, in case one of the following measures are taken:

Mitigation 1: a more stringent regulation and implementation on wastewater discharges into the river lowers the concentration of Adenoviruses by half.

Mitigation 2: swimming is permitted only one day a week. This would reduce the number of hazardous events and, therefore, it would lower the probability of infection at the end of the summer season.

Mitigation 3: allowing only for activities above water (ex. canoeing) would likely reduce the ingestion of contaminated water (letâ€™s assume to 7ml) and, therefore, risk of illness.

Going back to the previous steps, we could estimate each mitigation and evaluate which one could be more effective.

Conclusion

We hope this quick introduction has given a general understanding of the QMRA methodology.

For a detailed explanation of the methodology look at the book Quantitative Microbial Risk Assessment by Haas, Rose and Gerba. For more concise information, there is a QMRAwiki page

A review by Van Abel and Taylor on QMRA done in Sub-Saharan Africa gives an overview of limitations, approaches and differences that can be taken.

For a comparison with epidemiological data and for a clear straightforward application of the method, read this article by Mara, Sleigh, Blumenthal.

Lastly, keep in mind that, as any numerical modelling tool, the results are highly dependent on the quality of the data and assumptions.If you are curious to see how different input data can change results, read this article where we run a QMRA using two models: one using field data and one using published literature.
